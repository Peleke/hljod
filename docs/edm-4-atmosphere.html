EDM.4: the 808, dark chords, and space

Your EDM.3 capstone was ten layers of DnB at 170bpm. We’re dropping the tempo. Different speed, different genre, same tools. The target is phonk.

01The 808

In DnB, the bass was a reese—two detuned sawtooths. Phonk bass is simpler. A sine wave with a long tail:

sine with decay

A sine wave that rings and dies. That’s an 808 bass. The Roland TR-808 drum machine used this exact circuit for its kick: an oscillator that decays. Producers started pitching it down and holding it longer. The kick became the bass.

Give it a pattern. Two notes from C minor:

808 in c minor

C, then E-flat. Root and minor third. The bass carries melody now, not just weight.

808 bass

A sine oscillator shaped by a decay envelope. Long tail, low pitch. The note carries the melody. The envelope carries the weight. Named after the Roland TR-808.

Distortion

Clean 808s work. Distorted 808s bite:

distorted 808

.distort() clips the sine wave, adding harmonics. The bass gets grittier. A clean sine is a sub. A distorted sine is a phonk 808.

tweak it

Try .distort(0.1) for subtle warmth. .distort(0.8) for aggressive clipping. Add .crush(8) after distort for bit-crushing—a different kind of grit.

rabbit hole: the TR-808 circuit

The drum machine nobody wanted

Roland released the TR-808 in 1980. It was supposed to sound like real drums. It didn’t. The kick was a sine wave that decayed exponentially—a bridged-T oscillator circuit. Real kick drums don’t sound like that. Roland discontinued it in 1983.

The waveform

The 808 kick produces a decaying sine:

Orange: the actual waveform. Dashed: the exponential envelope. Same shape as note("c2").s("sine").decay(0.5).sustain(0). Same math, not an approximation.

Memphis

In the early 1990s, Memphis producers (DJ Paul, Juicy J, Three 6 Mafia) started using the 808 kick as a melodic bass instrument. They tuned it, pitched it, ran patterns with it. The kick stopped being percussion and became the foundation. That lineage runs straight through trap and into phonk.

Different decays

short, medium, long decay

Short decay: a kick. Medium: a bass hit. Long: a sub note. Same oscillator, different envelope. The decay knob decides what instrument this is.

02Space

Every sound so far is dry. No reflections, no echoes. Flat.

Reverb

A clap, raw:

dry clap

The same clap in a room:

reverbed clap

.room() is how much reverb. .size() is how big the space. The clap has a tail now. It sounds like it happened somewhere.

reverb

Simulated reflections of sound in a space. .room(amount) sets how much signal enters the reverb. .size(dimension) sets the room size. Dry = original signal. Wet = reverb signal.

Delay

Reverb is many reflections blurred. Delay is distinct echoes:

delayed cowbell

.delay() sets the echo level. .delaytime() sets the gap between echoes. .delayfeedback() controls how many times it repeats. The cowbell multiplies itself rhythmically.

delay

Discrete repetitions of a sound, spaced in time. .delay(amount) = wet level. .delaytime(seconds) = gap between echoes. .delayfeedback(amount) = how many echoes. Above 0.8, the echoes can run away.

tweak it

Try .delaytime(0.25) for quarter-note echoes. .delayfeedback(0.7) for a longer tail. Stack both effects: add .room(0.3).size(0.6) to the delayed cowbell. Reverb on the echoes.

rabbit hole: signal flow

Wet and dry

The signal splits. The dry path is the original sound, unchanged. The wet path runs through the effect (reverb or delay). The mixer blends them. .room(0.5) means 50% wet, 50% dry.

Delay feedback

Delay has a feedback loop: the output of the delay feeds back into its own input. Each pass through the loop is quieter. At .delayfeedback(0.5), each echo is half the volume of the previous one. At 0.9, echoes barely die. Above 1.0, they grow—runaway feedback.

Why bass stays dry

Same physics as why bass stays centered. Low frequencies create muddy, indistinct reverb tails. Reverb on a 50Hz sine just sounds like rumble. Keep the 808 dry. Put reverb on claps, cowbells, pads. Sounds with high-frequency content that reverb can shape.

03The Chord

Everything so far is one note at a time. What happens when you play two?

Two notes

C and G together. Comma inside brackets means “play at the same time”:

Now C and E-flat:

Three pairs. Three different moods. The notes are doing something to each other. Which pair felt heaviest? Which felt like it wanted to go somewhere?

What the shapes tell you

The orange wave is C. The blue wave is whatever you clicked. The green wave is both added together.

Click C+G. The green composite repeats cleanly. Click C+Gb. The composite writhes. The simpler the frequency ratio between two notes, the more regular the composite wave. 3:2 (the fifth) repeats every 2 cycles of C and 3 cycles of G. 45:32 (the tritone) takes far longer to repeat, if it visually repeats at all.

Your ear tracks this. Regular composites register as stable. Irregular composites register as tension. Click through all 13 notes and watch the green line.

In context: same melody, different roots

The keyboard shows isolated pairs. This editor puts them in motion. The melody stays the same. The bass note changes every cycle:

cycling roots under one melody

Six cycles, six bass notes, one melody. Each root recolors the same four notes above it.

Stack the roots: a chord appears

Three of those bass notes at once:

three roots stacked = chord

The chord is all the pairs at once. One root gave you a relationship. Three roots give you a context.

One holds, one moves

Hold C in the bass. Let another voice move over it:

drone + melody

The C holds still. The top voice moves: Eb, G, F, Eb. Every note in the melody sounds different because of the C underneath it. The held note changes the meaning of the moving one.

Change the anchor. Same melody, different bass note:

different anchor, same melody

Same four notes on top. Completely different feel. The anchor decides the mood. The melody is the same — what’s underneath it changed everything.

tweak it

Try note("g2") as the anchor. Or note("bb2"). The melody doesn’t change. Your experience of it does.

Three notes

Stack all three from the pairs — C, Eb, G. Together at once:

c minor chord

Dark. C and Eb give it weight. C and G give it width. Three notes, one mood. That’s a chord.

chord / triad

Three or more notes sounding at once. A triad has three: root, third, fifth. C minor = C (root), Eb (third), G (fifth). The Eb is what makes it dark. Swap it for E natural and the same chord turns bright — that’s C major.

pad

A sustained chord sound. Slow attack, long sustain, reverb. It fills background, sets mood. In the mini-notation, commas inside brackets mean “play simultaneously”: [c3,eb3,g3].

A voice over a chord

The chord holds. A melody moves on top:

chord + melody

Same idea as the drone, but now the anchor is a full chord instead of a single note. The melody has more context. More to agree or disagree with. This is where we’re heading — Phase 2 builds this properly.

The progression

One chord loops. A progression moves. Four chords, one per bar:

cm → ab → bb → g

Cm → Ab → Bb → G. Four bars, then loop. Each chord shifts the mood. The G major at the end pulls hard back to the top. Its B natural against the C minor key creates the pull. That’s what makes you want to hear the loop again.

chord progression

A sequence of chords that creates harmonic movement. This one is i–VI–VII–V in C minor. Roman numerals show the relationship to the key. Uppercase = major chord. Lowercase = minor. The V (G major) creates tension. The i (Cm) resolves it.

The bass has a job

The progression has four chords. The 808 from Section 01 plays one note at a time. What note should it play?

Go back to the drone idea. When C was underneath the melody, it felt like home. When Ab was underneath, the ground shifted. The bass note decided the mood. In a progression, the bass note needs to shift WITH the chords or the ground stays in the wrong place.

Simplest version: the bass plays the root of whatever chord is happening. Cm chord = C in the bass. Ab chord = Ab in the bass.

bass roots matching chords

The bass and the chords agree. Each bar has the same root in both layers. That alignment is what makes it sound like one piece of music instead of two things happening at the same time.

Bass as melody

One root per bar works. But the 808 can do more. It can move WITHIN each bar while still anchoring to the chord:

808 + pad

The bass still starts on each chord’s root. But now it moves between hits. C bar gets C and Eb. Ab bar gets Ab and C. The roots anchor. The other notes create motion inside each bar. The 808 went from a kick to a melody.

tweak it

Strip it back to note("<c2 ab2 bb2 g2>") and hear the difference. Then try adding a fifth to the Cm bar: [c2 ~ g2 ~ c2 ~ eb2 ~].

Beyond roots

The bass plays C for the C minor chord. That’s the root — the bottom of the chord. But the chord has three notes:

The root is the anchor. The top note — G — is the fifth. It’s the other strong note in the chord. Put both in the bass and the foundation gets wider:

root + fifth

The bass alternates between root and fifth. C and G for the C minor bar. Ab and Eb for Ab. Each pair is drawn from the chord above it. More motion, still stable.

Approach notes

One more idea. The last note of each bar can aim at the next bar’s root — one note below, sliding up into it:

G is one note below Ab. The bass leans into the next chord. It creates a pull — you hear the bar ending and the next one arriving.

root + fifth + approach

The last note of each bar is one step below the next bar’s root. G slides up to Ab. A slides up to Bb. F# slides up to G. B slides up to C (back to the top). The bass pulls you forward through the progression.

bass movement

Root = the chord’s anchor note. Fifth = the top note of the triad, reinforces the root. Approach note = one note below the next root, creates forward motion into the next bar. Root for stability. Fifth for width. Approach for momentum.

tweak it

Change the approach notes to come from ABOVE instead of below: try a2 before ab2 (one note above, sliding down). Different tension. Or try TWO approach notes: [bb2 ~ f2 ~ bb2 d2 c2 a2] — the bass walks down into the next root.

rabbit hole: why minor sounds dark

Major vs minor

The difference is one semitone. A major third is 4 semitones above the root (C to E). A minor third is 3 (C to Eb). That single semitone shift changes the entire character of the chord.

Hear it

c major then c minor

First bar: C major (C, E, G). Second bar: C minor (C, Eb, G). Only the middle note drops by one semitone. The mood flips.

The V chord

In the progression Cm–Ab–Bb–G, three chords come from C natural minor (Cm, Ab, Bb). The G major doesn’t. It borrows from C harmonic minor, which raises the seventh degree (Bb → B natural). That B natural creates a leading tone—it wants to resolve up to C. The tension is physical. Your ear expects the resolution. That’s why the loop works.

history

The 808 was a commercial failure. Memphis rap turned it into the most important instrument in modern music.

Roland TR-808 (1980–1983)

Ikutaro Kakehashi founded Roland in 1972. The TR-808 used analog synthesis (not samples) to generate drum sounds—a design choice driven by cost, not aesthetics. It couldn’t sound like real drums. Musicians hated it. Roland made about 12,000 units and discontinued production. Then Marvin Gaye used it on “Sexual Healing” (1982), Afrika Bambaataa used it on “Planet Rock” (1982), and it became the backbone of electro, freestyle, and early hip-hop.

Memphis rap (1991–1997)

DJ Paul (Paul Beauregard) and Juicy J (Jordan Houston) started making beats in North Memphis as teenagers, dubbing tapes and selling them out of car trunks. Equipment was whatever they could afford—often an 808 or SP-1200. The lo-fi quality wasn’t a choice. They used the 808 kick as a tuned bass instrument, running melodic patterns that carried the entire low end. Three 6 Mafia’s Mystic Stylez (1995) codified the sound: dark samples (horror movies, soul records), heavy 808, reverb-drenched vocals. Tommy Wright III, Gangsta Pat, and Koopsta Knicca operated in the same ecosystem.

Phonk revival (2013–present)

DJ Smokey and SpaceGhostPurrp began sampling Three 6 Mafia vocals over new production in the early 2010s, calling it “phonk.” Kaito Shoma’s “Scary Garry” (2016) added the signature blown-out cowbell, launching “drift phonk.” By 2021, phonk had crossed into mainstream TikTok culture. The aesthetic is deliberately lo-fi—an homage to the Memphis tapes’ constraints.

Sources: Reid, “History of Roland” (2004); Miller, “Memphis Rap” in Oxford Handbook of Hip-Hop Music (2022); Bein, “How Phonk Went From Memphis Rap Underground to Global Phenomenon,” Billboard (2022).
